What am I looking at?

You're looking at a system I made because I was unsatisfied with the Challenge Rating system present in the Dungeons & Dragons 5th-edition rules. Lots of stuff I could mention---but I won't. It would turn into a hurricane of nitpicking. Instead, I'll tell you why you should consider using The Next Level, the system I came up with. Here's the pitch:

  • Creature Level = Player Level: A 3rd-level creature is a reasonable encounter for a single level 3 player. Four players? Four creatures.
  • A 3-Encounter Adventuring Day: Down from 6, a better fit for most playstyles, but with enough leeway that combatants won't go full nova on each other all the time.
  • 3-Turn Encounters: Long enough for tactical and exciting encounters, short enough to prevent the dreaded Combat Slog.
  • Consistent Rewards: The threshold to the next level is always 1000 XP. It's easy to hand out roleplaying/exploration/quest XP like this, since there's no weird XP thresholds you have to think about. It doesn't matter whether you're playing with players of level 3 or level 13---the numbers are the same.

Using the calculator

On the surface this system is mostly similar to the one in the DM's Guide: you'll calculate a creature's offensive and defensive abilities, and calculate their Level from that. I plugged the numbers into the interactive calculator to the left so you can focus on what matters: the stats, and by extension, the fun at the table. Plug some numbers in the calculator, leave the boxes whose values you want to know empty, and they will be filled in automagically. Play around with it and it should become clear. Hover your mouse over a box for a short description on its contents.

Level

Just that: a creature's level says something about how strong it is. Check out the Building Encounters section for more information, but a good rule of thumb is that one player should fight one creature of their own level for a medium-difficulty encounter.

Hit Points

Hit Points say something about a creature's durability. Make sure to account for any special traits a creature may have when drafting a creature's stat block:

  • A wraith resists most damage, so it's effective hit points are doubled.
  • Trolls have regeneration---so add extra hit points for every round of the fight. (3 rounds, usually)
  • Zombies have a trait that may let them get back up after a lethal blow. In cases like this, I assume it'll give them one extra round, and I multiply their hit points by that amount (4/3) before punching it in.

Armor Class

Depending on a creature's final level, an AC of 18 might be anywhere from high to just middling. I, myself convert saving throws to 'Ability Score ACs' for faster play (attack with Attack Bonus, defend with 14 + Bonus to Saving Throws). I've found that putting 75% AC and 25% the average of a creature's Dexterity, Constitution, and Wisdom Ability Score AC works pretty well.

  • If a creature can force disadvantage on attack rolls/advantage on saving throws (e.g. invisibility), I account for that here (+2 to +3 bonus to the relevant stat).
  • Creatures that can attack without being open to counterattacks (ranged attacks and flight; counterspell) should probably also get a +2 bonus or so.

Damage

Damage, without hit ratio factored in. If a creature has multiple actions it is likely to use (like a breath weapon), take the average of the damage it deals over 3 rounds of combat.

  • Gnolls get a free Bite attack when they kill something; I assume an ability like this is used at least once per encounter, so I add 1/3 of their Bite to the gnoll's total damage output.
  • Orcs don't have much in the way of damage, but their Aggressive trait makes it easy to close in on their targets, and hard for their targets to get away. I'd count that as a single extra round: multiply their damage by 4/3.
  • Some spells and abilities hit multiple creatures. The DMG suggests to assume such an ability hits two creatures, and to assume saving throws are automatically failed. I assume the same.

Attack Bonus

Or Saving Throw DC, whichever you prefer (just subtract 8 for the corresponding Attack Bonus).

  • Kobolds, Wolves, Sahuagin, and several other creatures get easy advantage on their attacks. So do invisible creatures. I usually count that as a +2--+3 bonus and leave it at that.
  • Some spells and skills, like bless, increase a creature's to hit bonus.

Building a creature

Enter numbers you already have in the tool to the left. A level too if you've got one in mind---otherwise the tool will compute it from other values you put in. Once you've got all five, turn them into a creature, tweaking their statistics to account for special abilities they have.

Creating a boss creature

Boss creatures are often supposed to be tough and dangerous, but in 5th-edition D&D that often translates to glass cannons and swingy encounters. I like to replace the Legendary Action system in the Monster Manual with multiple initiative counts in a single round---effectively splitting a boss's multiattack and movement over 2 or more turns. Doing it this way means their damage is spread over the whole round, so players can adapt. It also means your boss is vulnerable to hold person and other cool spells your players have, but they break out quicker than most normal creatures do.

Statting up world-destroying creatures

Play around with the calculator and you'll figure out that its levels range from -3 to 30: more than enough for most uses. Some really overpowered creatures, like the Monster Manual's Tarrasque, don't really fit the idea of this system though. At that scale of play the numbers kind of break down regardless of what you do. If you are in need of a world-destroying titan though, it's doable: just run it as if it were multiple creatures at once, and tweak HP/damage/XP accordingly.

Building encounters and awarding XP

I suggest using the table below when you're building an encounter. It's similar to Pathfinder 2e's system and the Unearthed Arcana article on 3-Pillar XP: the stronger your players get, the less XP is awarded for an encounter with the same creature. For party level, I suggest taking the average level. I award XP to players individually, though you could also award lower-levelled players double the XP if you want them to level up more quickly.

As for building encounters, that system is still in the playtesting phase; but the gist of it is already here. One player should be able to take on one creature of their own level as a medium-difficulty encounter. That's 80 XP; multiply that by the number of players in your party and you have an XP budget. Three encounters per day gives you 240 XP per player per day, if you care about that; I tend to leave it my players which encounters they'll fight and which ones they'll flee.

I also award XP for roleplaying, exploration, and general fun. I separate those into minor, moderate, and major categories. I award 20 XP for funny quips, and great descriptions. 80 XP I award for great roleplaying, usually in addition to Inspiration tokens. I tend to reserve 120 XP for when party completes a story arc, and for exceptional roleplaying.

From the Monster Manual: Hell Hounds

Let's use the Hell Hound in the SRD as guinea pig. A quick overview of their stat block tells me that these dogs aren't too tactically sophisticated: their Intelligence is 6. With a high Wisdom and proficiency in Perception, I'd probably use Hell Hounds as hunting dogs for fiends, or other evil-aligned creatures who could find (and tame!) one.

Hell Hounds have a damage immunity: fire. Apart from fireball and lower-levelled fire-elemental spells, I think it's unlikely for players to base their damage output entirely on fire in particular, so I wouldn't add this to their Hit Point count. That leaves them with 45.5. hit points if you count up the average value for their Hit Dice as listed.

Their AC is a bit more involved. A Hell Hound's actual Armor Class is 15; I factor in their major saving throw bonuses, which are +1, +2, and +1 for their Dexterity, Constitution, and Wisdom, respectively. It's easy to turn these into AC-like values: just add 14 to each of them (hover your mouse over the AC box for a full explanation). Putting all of that together, we end up with the following value in the AC box:

15 0.75 + (15 + 16 + 15) / 3 0.25 = 15 0.75 + (15 + 16 + 15) / 12 = *15.1

Next, let's calculate their damage output. A Hell Hound's bite attack deals 7.5 + 7 = 14.5 damage on average. Their fire breath is a bit more involved---as is usual for attacks that hit a sizable area, we'll assume two targets are damaged. Each target takes 21 damage, so 21 + 21, which is 42 damage in all. That action has a 1 in 3 chance of recharching, though, so we'll assume it is only used once. Averaging those numbers, we end up with (14.5 + 14.5 + 42) / 3 = 23.7 damage per turn.

Calculating their Attack Bonus should be easier. Hell Hounds have the pack tactics trait. According to the DMG, pack tactics is equivalent to a +1 bonus to attack rolls, so we end up with a +6 bonus to Attack Rolls. You can just fill that in, but we can also factor in the hit ratio of their fire breath action, if we really want to. It has a DC 12 saving throw, which is based on the Hell Hound's Constitution (8 + their Constitution modifier + proficiency). Subtracting that static 8 leaves us with a +4 bonus to hit. We already figured out that the fire breath action was likely going to be used one in three times, so we'll average it the same way: (5 + 5 + 4) / 3 = a +4.7 bonus to attack rolls on average.

Plug in all those numbers, and the calculator will spit out the right level: 5.8, which rounds up to a level 6 creature.

Creating a boss encounter: Cattle-Plague

It is early summer in Hylloch, a small town on the edge of the Greenlake. The harvest is shaping up to be a good one, and Lord Forsythe has called for woodcutters to cut down accessible parts of the neighboring Bolderwood. The extra space is used for a corral to keep cattle from various farmers who live around town. It is surrounded by a sturdy fence, and the gate is locked each evening by a man from Forsythe manor, Fredrick. When one day, Shock! A cow's hind is found, cut clean in two, the front half nowhere to be found. Who did this?

This could be a fun intro-level quest to defeat an ankheg or a similar, beetle-like insectoid monster, raised by a crazy hermit who lives in the woods. The one in the Monster Manual is pretty boring, though, so let's spice it up a little. Assuming a 1st-level party of four players, we'd need a creature of level 6 for a Medium encounter (280 XP). There is a significant bump in ability at levels 5, 11, and 17 for players and creatures though, so let's go with a conservative estimate of 5 (200 XP).

I usually like my bosses to be dynamic encounters, so our boss should have multiple actions that shake up everyone's expectations. As a start, let's give it a bite attack that also inflicts a grapple; if we throw in the ankheg's digestive acid, that should provide incentive to get out, fast. Let's also keep the acid breath weapon, but it shouldn't deal much damage; our party is only 1st-level, after all. Instead, let's change it to corrode the target's armor. That might seem harsh---but since this is the boss of the adventure, the characters will get a big quest reward afterwards as compensation. Finally, we'll give it two dynamic reactions: once it is struck for a sufficient amount of damage in a single blow, its carapace breaks, spilling pools of acid everywhere. And once its hit points start to dwindle, it will burrow underground, dragging grappled characters with it during its flight.

Let's start by entering what we already know: a level (5). That gives us some numbers to work with. A giant insect is probably not very dextrous. It will probably have a decent Constitution score, and a lower-than-average Wisdom. Let's arbitrarily choose a -1, +2, and -1 modifier, respectively, giving it an average saving throw 'AC' of 13.7. We'd need an AC of 16.8 to compensate. Let's do that, and keep the beast's underbelly largely unprotected if players manage to flip it over. All that together comes to an effective AC of 16: exactly what the calculator recommended. Let's fix the beast's hit points slightly below the recommended value at 52, which comes out to exactly 7 hit dice. (The rules recommend a d10 hit die for large creatures.)

Then we'll just need to figure out how it attacks. An effective attack bonus of 7 is high for a 1st-level party, though; let's drop that to 6. That also gives us a little bit more damage to spread around the various attacks we've got planned. First, let's get the basic attack action and the acid breath out of the way:

Bite. Melee Weapon Attack: +5 to hit, reach 5 ft., 1 target. Hit: 5 (2d4+2) piercing damage, and the target is grappled (escape DC 12).

Acid Spray (Recharge 5-6). One target Cattle-Plague can see within 30 feet must make a DC 12 Dexterity saving throw. On a failure, it takes 2d6 acid damage. If the target is wearing armor, it corrodes and their armor class is permanently reduced by 1. If this reduces the armor's AC to 10, it is destroyed. On a successful saving throw, the target only takes half damage, and their armor is not corroded.

Reducing a target's armor indirectly raises the probability of our attacks hitting; so indirectly, our attack bonus is still somewhere around 6. Let's also add in a trait to codify the beast's digestive juices:

Acid Saliva. A target grappled by Cattle-Plague takes 1d4 acid damage at the start of each of their turns.

That puts our damage output at (2 * (2d4+2 + 1d4) + 2d6) / 3 = 8.7 damage per turn. So we have 11.4 - 8.7 = 2.7 damage left over for the acid pools. So let's throw in another trait:

Splintering Carapace. Once Cattle-Plague takes a total of 20 damage, its carapace splinters, spilling acid in a 5-feet circle centered on itself. The acid deals 1d4 acid damage whenever a creature enters the area for the first time on its turn or starts its turn there. The acid disappears after 1 minute.

Finally, let's give it a flight reaction:

Flight. Once Cattle-Plague drops to 17 hit points or below, it can immediately burrow up to its speed, dragging creatures it is grappling with it.

And we're done!
